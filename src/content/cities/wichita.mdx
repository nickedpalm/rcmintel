## How to Choose a Medical Billing Service in Wichita

- **Check certifications before anything else.** A credentialed biller carries a CHBME (from HBMA), a CPC (from AAPC), or a CCS (from AHIMA) — these aren't vanity letters. They signal that someone has passed a standardized exam on coding accuracy and compliance. If a firm can't tell you which certifications their coders hold, that's your answer.

- **Ask for specialty-specific experience.** A biller who's spent five years on orthopedic claims codes differently than one who's handled primary care. Wichita's largest employers — Wesley Medical Center, Ascension Via Christi — run high volumes of complex inpatient claims. The independent practices that receive their specialty referrals need billers who understand that ecosystem.

- **Demand clean claim rate data upfront.** Industry average sits around 94–96%. If a firm hedges on this number or calls it "proprietary," walk. Any credible medical billing service tracks this religiously and will show you benchmarks from comparable practices without being asked twice.

- **Understand the Kansas payer mix reality.** KanCare — Kansas's Medicaid managed care program — operates through three MCOs: Aetna Better Health, Sunflower Health Plan, and United Healthcare Community Plan. Your biller needs to know these contracts specifically. Someone who learned their trade in Texas may not know KanCare's prior authorization quirks cold.

- **Get a straight answer on denial management.** Billing and *collecting* are two different jobs. Ask what percentage of denied claims they appeal, and what their overturn rate is. If they submit claims and stop there, you're leaving money on the table every single month.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask any prospective firm to walk you through their process for handling a payer downcoding — specifically a 99214 coded down to a 99213. How they answer tells you more than any sales deck.

## What to Expect

Medical billing services in Wichita typically run between **$500 and $5,000 per month**, with the range driven by practice size, claim volume, and specialty complexity. Most firms price on a percentage of collections — typically 4–9% — rather than flat fees, so a solo internist and a ten-provider cardiology group will land in very different places. Onboarding takes two to four weeks for a clean handoff; expect a brief dip in cash flow as the new biller rebuilds payer enrollment and ERA setup.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest option almost always costs more in the long run. A biller charging 4% who lets AR age past 90 days will erode far more revenue than one charging 7% who chases every denial. Ask for net collection ratio data, not just the sticker rate.
