## How to Choose a Medical Billing Service in Denver

- **Verify credentials before the conversation goes any further.** Ask for CHBME, CPC, or CCS certifications upfront. Any shop worth your money will have them on file and won't hesitate to share. Colorado doesn't license medical billers at the state level, so certification is the only external accountability mechanism you've got.
- **Match specialization to your practice type.** A biller who's great at orthopedic claims may be lost in behavioral health. Denver has a high concentration of mental health and addiction recovery practices — if that's your niche, find a biller who already knows Colorado's Medicaid behavioral health billing quirks inside out.
- **Ask for their clean claim rate and days-in-AR benchmarks.** Industry standard is 95%+ clean claim rate and under 35 days in AR. If they can't produce those numbers for current clients, keep walking.
- **Clarify Colorado-specific payer contracts.** Colorado's Medicaid program (Health First Colorado) and the dominant commercial payers — Cigna, Anthem, Aetna — each have their own claims submission quirks and appeal windows. A local or regionally experienced biller knows these by feel; a national shop may need a learning curve you pay for.
- **Get a denial management SLA in writing.** Denials are where revenue goes to die. A good service will specify turnaround time for resubmissions — 48 to 72 hours is the benchmark. Vague answers here are a red flag.

> **Pro Tip:** Denver's altitude isn't the only thing that can leave you breathless — audit a prospective biller's net collection ratio (NCR) for a practice similar to yours in size and specialty. NCR below 95% means money is slipping through the cracks, and it's usually the billing service's fault, not the payers'.

## What to Expect

Medical billing services in Denver typically run $500–$5,000 per month, with most small-to-midsize practices landing somewhere in the $1,000–$2,500 range depending on claim volume and specialty complexity. Onboarding usually takes two to four weeks — expect a practice management system audit, EHR integration, and a credentialing review before claims start flowing. First-month performance metrics should be available within 30 days.

> **Reality Check:** Percentage-of-collections pricing (usually 4–9%) sounds appealing because it aligns incentives, but it can cost you more than a flat monthly fee once volume scales up. Run the math against your average monthly collections before signing. A practice billing $80K/month at a 7% rate pays $5,600 — well above what a flat-fee contract would run.

The city's status as a regional referral hub for the Mountain West means practices here often deal with out-of-state insurance plans and complex multi-payer scenarios — exactly the kind of billing environment where an inexperienced service will cost you more than they save.
