## How to Choose a Medical Billing Service in San Antonio

- **Verify certifications, not just claims.** Ask for the specific credential, the certifying body, and the certificate number. CHBME (from HBMA), CPC (AAPC), and CCS (AHIMA) are the gold standard. Anyone who tells you they're "certified" without naming the body is waving a red flag.
- **Ask about TRICARE and VA billing experience.** San Antonio is Military City USA — Brooke Army Medical Center, Lackland, and a massive active-duty and veteran population mean TRICARE and VA claims are a daily reality here. If your biller hasn't worked that payer mix before, your denial rate will prove it.
- **Request a clean claim rate and days-in-AR benchmark from a current client in your specialty.** Generic promises mean nothing. A real billing service will hand you a one-page performance summary without hesitation. If they hedge, walk.
- **Clarify who owns your data.** Some services lock your EHR and billing data into their platform. In Texas, that's a negotiation you want to have before onboarding, not during a contract dispute.
- **Make sure they know Texas Medicaid (TMHP).** The Texas Medicaid & Healthcare Partnership has its own portal, its own quirks, and its own claim submission process. Billers who primarily work other states learn this the hard way on your dime.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask for a 90-day performance review clause in your contract. Any service confident in their clean claim rates won't blink at it — and it gives you a clean exit if they miss benchmarks.

## What to Expect

Medical billing services in San Antonio typically run $500–$5,000 per month depending on your specialty, volume, and whether you're outsourcing coding alongside billing. Most established services charge a percentage of collections (typically 4–9%) rather than a flat fee, which aligns incentives — they get paid when you do. Expect onboarding to take 2–4 weeks for credentialing transfers and payer enrollment setup before claims start flowing cleanly.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest option almost always costs more in the long run. A biller charging 4% who lets your denial rate creep to 18% is destroying more revenue than the biller charging 7% who keeps denials under 5%. Net collection ratio — not price — is the number that matters.
