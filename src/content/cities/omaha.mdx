## How to Choose a Medical Billing Service in Omaha

- **Verify credentials before anything else.** Look for CHBME, CPC, or CCS certifications from HBMA, AAPC, or AHIMA respectively. Nebraska doesn't license medical billers at the state level, so certification is the only third-party accountability that exists. Ask to see proof — not just a logo on the website.
- **Ask specifically about Nebraska Medicaid (Heritage Health).** Heritage Health is the managed Medicaid program in Nebraska, and it has non-obvious timely filing rules and prior auth requirements that trip up billers who learned their craft in other states. If they go blank when you mention it, walk.
- **Demand a clean claim rate above 95%.** The industry average hovers around 90–92%. Any service unwilling to share this metric — or who hedges with "it depends on your specialty" — is hiding underperformance.
- **Understand their denial management workflow.** Billing is easy. Working denials is where money is actually recovered. Ask what percentage of denials they appeal, how fast, and what their overturn rate is. If they can't answer in numbers, they're not tracking it.
- **Negotiate a trial period with performance benchmarks.** Days in AR should be below 35 for most specialties. Net collection ratio should be above 95%. Build those into a 90-day review clause before you sign a long-term contract.

> **Pro Tip:** Omaha has a dense concentration of independent physician practices affiliated with Nebraska Medicine and CHI Health systems. If your practice bills under a hospital-based or academic group arrangement, confirm your billing service has handled facility-fee and split-billing scenarios — most boutique shops haven't.

## What to Expect

Medical billing services in Omaha typically run $500–2,500/month for small practices (1–3 providers) and $2,500–5,000/month for mid-size clinics, though some larger RCM firms price as a percentage of collections (usually 4–8%). Onboarding takes 2–4 weeks — expect to export your existing AR, hand over payer credentialing files, and run parallel billing for the first billing cycle to catch gaps.

> **Reality Check:** The percentage-of-collections model sounds aligned with your incentives, but it can backfire. Billers on percentage have a structural reason to prioritize easy wins (high-volume, low-complexity claims) and let complex denials age out. If your practice has a complicated payer mix or specialty procedures, a flat monthly fee with explicit SLAs on denial turnaround is usually the better deal.
